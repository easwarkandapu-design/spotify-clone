Subhas Chandra Bose

Subhas Chandra Bose, popularly known as Netaji, was one of the greatest freedom fighters of India. He was born on 23 January 1897 in Cuttack, Odisha. From a young age, he was intelligent, courageous, and deeply patriotic. He completed his education in India and later went to England for higher studies, where he passed the Indian Civil Services examination. However, he resigned from the job because he wanted to dedicate his life to the struggle for India’s independence from British rule. He joined the Indian National Congress and became an important leader, inspiring millions of Indians with his powerful speeches and strong leadership. During World War II, Subhas Chandra Bose formed the Indian National Army (INA), also known as Azad Hind Fauj, to fight against the British. His famous slogan, “Give me blood, and I will give you freedom,” motivated many young Indians to join the freedom movement. He also used the slogan “Jai Hind,” which later became a national slogan of India. Bose believed that freedom could not be achieved only through peaceful protests and therefore supported armed struggle against British rule. He traveled to countries like Germany and Japan to seek support for India’s independence. Although he is believed to have died in a plane crash in 1945, his death remains a mystery. Subhas Chandra Bose is remembered as a brave patriot whose sacrifice and dedication greatly contributed to India’s freedom struggle.

Bhagat Singh

Bhagat Singh was one of the greatest revolutionary freedom fighters of India. He was born on 28 September 1907 in Banga, Punjab (now in Pakistan). From his childhood, he was deeply inspired by the struggle for India’s independence and was greatly affected by the Jallianwala Bagh massacre. He was a brave, intelligent, and patriotic young man who wanted to free India from British rule. Bhagat Singh joined revolutionary organizations and worked with leaders like Chandra Shekhar Azad to fight against the British government. He believed that strong action was necessary to awaken the people of India and inspire them to fight for freedom. Bhagat Singh became famous for his fearless acts against British rule. Along with his companions Rajguru and Sukhdev, he protested against the death of Lala Lajpat Rai by killing British officer Saunders. Later, Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt threw harmless bombs in the Central Legislative Assembly to protest against unfair British laws and shouted the slogan “Inquilab Zindabad,” meaning “Long Live the Revolution.” He was arrested and sentenced to death by the British government. Bhagat Singh was hanged on 23 March 1931 at the young age of 23 along with Rajguru and Sukhdev. He is remembered as a symbol of courage, sacrifice, and patriotism, and his contribution to India’s freedom struggle continues to inspire millions of people.
